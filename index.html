Lesson 1.1: Three Kinds of Intelligence

Chapter 1: What Intelligence Really Is - Your First AI Experiments
Lesson Day | Duration: 50 minutes

Lesson Overview

Lesson Type Lesson Day (Concept Introduction)
Duration 50 minutes
Chapter Chapter 1 (Lesson 1 of 7-8)
Prerequisites None (First lesson of course)
Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Define intelligence and recognize it exists in multiple forms
  • Distinguish between human, animal, and artificial intelligence with specific examples
  • Identify key strengths and limitations of each intelligence type
  • Explain why different intelligence types are suited for different tasks
  • Articulate the difference between Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Artificial Consciousness (AC)
  • Begin forming hypotheses about whether AI "thinks" and what that means
Key Concepts
  • Intelligence: The capacity to acquire, process, and apply information
  • Human Intelligence: Flexible thinking with strengths in generalization, context, creativity, and adaptation
  • Animal Intelligence: Specialized excellence evolved for specific survival tasks
  • Artificial Intelligence (AI): Pattern recognition at scale, processing massive data without understanding meaning
  • Artificial Consciousness (AC): Theoretical concept of machines with subjective experience (not demonstrated)
  • Generalization: Understanding concepts from limited examples and applying them to new situations

Detailed Lesson Plan

Opening Hook & Course Introduction (7-8 minutes)

Welcome to Understanding AI

Teacher Action: Begin with energizing introduction to the course.

"Welcome to Understanding AI. This course is different from any other class you've taken. You won't be writing code or solving complex equations. Instead, you're going to become AI researchers. You'll conduct experiments, document observations like scientists, and investigate one of the most important questions of our time: What is AI really doing? Does it think? Does it understand? Or is it something else entirely?"

Set Expectations:

  • "Throughout this semester, you'll work with an AI system called Alia - our AI Learning Assistant"
  • "You'll conduct hands-on experiments to test AI's capabilities and limitations"
  • "You'll keep a field journal documenting your discoveries"
  • "You'll develop a creative writing project in collaboration with AI"
  • "Most importantly: You'll form your own conclusions based on evidence you gather"

The Opening Question

Display or write on board:

"When you talk to Siri, Alexa, or ChatGPT - does it feel like you're communicating with something intelligent? Does it feel conscious, like there's someone 'in there'?"

Give 30 seconds silent thinking time, then quick popcorn responses.

Students will have varied intuitions:
  • "Yes, it feels like talking to someone"
  • "No, I know it's just a computer program"
  • "Sometimes it seems smart, sometimes it's obviously fake"
  • "It depends on what I ask it"
Don't resolve the question yet. Say: "Interesting - we have different intuitions. Keep that question in mind as we start today's lesson. By the end of the semester, you'll have gathered evidence to answer it yourself."

Transition to core lesson:

"To investigate whether AI thinks, we first need to understand what thinking and intelligence actually are. Today's lesson explores three different kinds of intelligence: human, animal, and artificial. Pay attention to what each type does well and what it struggles with."

Core Lesson: Three Kinds of Intelligence (20-25 minutes)

Choose Your Delivery Method

Option A: Class Video Viewing (Recommended for Day 1)

  • Play Lesson 1.1 video for entire class
  • Students watch together on projected screen
  • Advantage: Shared experience, easier to pause for questions, no tech issues
  • Duration: Approximately 20 minutes
  • Teacher role: Pause at key moments for quick checks (see suggested pause points below)

Option B: Individual Reading

  • Students read Lesson 1.1 content independently
  • Provide digital or print version of reading
  • Advantage: Students work at own pace, develops reading comprehension
  • Duration: 20-25 minutes (varies by reader)
  • Teacher role: Circulate, answer questions, monitor engagement

Option C: Individual Video Viewing (with headphones)

  • Students watch Lesson 1.1 video on individual devices
  • Requires headphones OR use closed captions
  • Advantage: Students can pause/rewind as needed, multimedia learning
  • Duration: Approximately 20 minutes
  • Teacher role: Monitor progress, help with technical issues
For first day of course, Option A (class video viewing) is recommended. This creates shared experience, allows you to gauge student engagement, and establishes norms for how the class operates. You can transition to more independent reading/viewing in later lessons once routines are established.

If Using Option A (Class Video Viewing) - Suggested Pause Points:

Pause Point 1: After Human Intelligence section (approx. 5 minutes in)

Quick check: "Think-pair-share - Turn to a partner and explain in your own words: What makes human intelligence 'flexible'? Use the chair example or your own example."

Give 60 seconds, then resume video

Pause Point 2: After Animal Intelligence section (approx. 10 minutes in)

Quick check: "What's one example of animal intelligence that surprised you? Why?"

Take 2-3 quick responses, then resume video

Pause Point 3: After the bird-in-a-hat example (approx. 16 minutes in)

Quick check: "Why would AI classify the image as 'hat' instead of 'bird'? What does this reveal?"

Brief discussion (1 minute), then resume video

These pause points keep students engaged during video viewing and provide formative assessment. Don't pause more than suggested - you want momentum. Save deeper discussion for after the full lesson.

If Using Option B or C (Individual Reading/Viewing):

Before students begin:

"As you read/watch, pay special attention to:

  • Examples that surprise you
  • Differences between the three intelligence types
  • Strengths and limitations of each type
  • The difference between AI and AC (Artificial Consciousness)

You'll have about 20-25 minutes. Work at your own pace."

While students work independently:
  • Circulate to monitor engagement
  • Answer individual questions quietly
  • Note which students finish early (provide extension activity)
  • Note which students struggle with pacing (may need reading support)
  • Give 5-minute and 2-minute warnings before discussion time

Discussion & Application (15 minutes)

Activity 1: Comparing Intelligence Types (8 minutes)

Teacher Action: Create three-column chart on board

Human Intelligence Animal Intelligence Artificial Intelligence
Students fill in key strengths Students fill in key strengths Students fill in key strengths
Students fill in limitations Students fill in limitations Students fill in limitations

Guide students to complete the chart through class discussion.

Expected responses:

Human Intelligence

  • Strengths: Generalization from few examples, understanding context/nuance, creative problem-solving, adapting to novel situations
  • Limitations: Slow processing, gets tired, makes calculation errors, imperfect memory, can't process millions of data points simultaneously

Animal Intelligence

  • Strengths: Specialized excellence (navigation, spatial memory, tool use, sensory processing far beyond human capability)
  • Limitations: Often narrow to specific survival tasks, limited generalization to unfamiliar problems

Artificial Intelligence

  • Strengths: Processes massive data quickly, recognizes subtle patterns, consistent performance without fatigue, multitasking at scale
  • Limitations: Recognizes patterns without understanding meaning, lacks common sense, can't truly adapt to completely new situations without retraining, struggles with context
The key insight students should reach: Each intelligence type excels at certain tasks but struggles with others. There's no "best" intelligence - it depends on the task.

Activity 2: The Tool Analogy (4 minutes)

Teacher Action: Revisit the lesson's key metaphor

"The lesson compared intelligence types to tools - like asking whether a hammer or screwdriver is 'better.' Why is this a good way to think about intelligence?"

Guide students to articulate:

  • Each tool (intelligence type) is designed for specific purposes
  • Using the wrong tool for a task leads to poor outcomes
  • We should match intelligence type to task requirements
  • Understanding strengths/limitations helps us choose wisely

Application question:

"If you needed to analyze 10 million financial transactions to find fraud patterns, which intelligence type would be best? Why?"

Answer: AI - can process massive data quickly and spot patterns humans would miss

"If you needed to understand why your friend seems upset even though they said 'I'm fine,' which intelligence type would be best? Why?"

Answer: Human - understands context, reads tone and body language, recognizes social nuance

"If you needed to navigate 10,000 miles across ocean without GPS, which intelligence type would excel?"

Answer: Animal (Arctic tern) - specialized navigation using sun, stars, and Earth's magnetic field

Activity 3: The Big Question (3 minutes)

Teacher Action: Return to course's central investigation

"The lesson says we'll investigate: 'What is AI really doing when it responds to us?' Based on what you learned today, what's your initial hypothesis? Does AI think? Does it understand?"

Take 3-4 student responses. Emphasize:

  • These are preliminary hypotheses - you'll test them through experiments
  • It's okay to be uncertain or change your mind as you gather evidence
  • The lesson distinguishes AI (demonstrated) from AC (theoretical) - keep this in mind
  • You'll meet Alia soon and start investigating these questions firsthand

Review Questions & Closing (5 minutes)

Completion of Review Questions

Teacher Action: Ensure all students complete the 5 review questions

Important timing note: The 5 review questions are embedded at the end of the lesson content. Fast readers/viewers will have already attempted these during the Core Lesson time. Slower students may not have reached them yet. This final 5 minutes ensures everyone completes them.

Teacher says: "Everyone should now have the 5 review questions open on your device. If you already answered them, take a moment to review your responses and revise if needed. If you haven't gotten to them yet, complete them now. You have 5 minutes."

Administration format:

  • Digital (recommended): Questions are embedded in digital lesson or separate form (Google Form, etc.)
  • Students can revise answers during this time
  • Questions remain "open" until end of class period
  • Teacher can see completion status in real-time (most digital platforms)

Alternative (if using paper):

  • Provide separate handout with 5 questions
  • Students complete and turn in before leaving
  • Note: Harder to allow revision with paper format
These are engagement/completion questions, not high-stakes assessment. For Lesson 1.1, all questions are multiple choice, making this accessible to all students on Day 1. Students should do well - this builds confidence and establishes the routine of completing review questions. Future lessons will incorporate short answer questions requiring written explanation.

Assessment

Formative Assessment

During Lesson:
  • Video pause points: Listen for student ability to explain concepts in own words
  • Chart completion: Can students identify accurate strengths/limitations of each intelligence type?
  • Tool analogy application: Do students correctly match intelligence type to task?
Review Questions:
  • Success indicator: 80%+ of students answer 4-5 questions correctly
  • Red flag: Many students miss Q3 (AI limitation) - may indicate need to emphasize "pattern without meaning" concept
  • Common confusion: Students conflate AI capability with AI understanding

Look For:

  • Can students distinguish between AI and AC (Artificial Consciousness)?
  • Do students recognize intelligence as task-specific rather than one-dimensional?
  • Are students beginning to think critically about AI capabilities vs. limitations?
  • Do students show curiosity about investigating AI more deeply?

Differentiation Strategies

For Students Who Need More Support

  • During independent reading: Provide guided reading questions to focus attention on key concepts
  • Visual learners: Create simple icons for each intelligence type (brain = human, paw = animal, circuit = AI)
  • Vocabulary support: Pre-teach: intelligence, generalization, specialized, artificial, consciousness
  • Simplified chart: Provide partially completed comparison chart with some examples filled in
  • Partner reading: Allow students to read with a buddy and discuss as they go

For Advanced Students

  • Extension question: "The lesson distinguishes AI from AC. Why do you think no one has created Artificial Consciousness yet? What might be required?"
  • Research task: Look up "AlphaGo" (mentioned in lesson) and prepare 1-minute explanation of how it learned
  • Philosophical challenge: "If AI can pass the Turing Test (fool humans into thinking it's conscious), does that mean it IS conscious?"
  • Early finishers: Read ahead to Lesson 1.2 or research one type of AI (visual, auditory, textual)

English Language Learners

  • Provide glossary of key terms with definitions and translations
  • Use visual examples heavily (images in lesson are helpful)
  • Allow extra time for reading if using Option B
  • Permit use of translation tools during reading
  • Pre-teach the metaphor/analogy concept (tool comparison may be unfamiliar)
  • Pair with bilingual buddy for discussion activities

Common Student Misconceptions

Misconception How to Address
"AI is conscious like humans" Emphasize lesson's distinction: AI (demonstrated) vs. AC (theoretical, not demonstrated). AI can behave intelligently without experiencing consciousness.
"If AI can do something well, it must understand it" Use AlphaGo example: plays Go brilliantly but doesn't understand why Go is a game, can't transfer skills to checkers. Performance ‚â† comprehension.
"Human intelligence is the best intelligence" Challenge with examples: AI analyzes data better, birds navigate better, dogs smell better. Best for what purpose?
"Animal intelligence is just instinct, not real intelligence" Highlight tool use, problem-solving, social learning examples. Crows planning multi-step solutions demonstrates reasoning, not just instinct.
"AI will become conscious eventually" The lesson doesn't make predictions about future. Currently AC is theoretical. We'll investigate what current AI actually does throughout the course.

Extension & Enrichment

If Students Finish Early

  • Journal prompt: "Describe one experience you've had with AI. Based on today's lesson, which strengths/limitations of AI did you observe?"
  • Creative challenge: Draw or diagram the three intelligence types with examples from your own life
  • Research task: Find one news article about AI. Does the article distinguish between AI and AC? Is it accurate based on what you learned?
  • Thought experiment: If you could choose one superpower from animal intelligence (echolocation like bats, navigation like terns, memory like squirrels), which would you choose and why?
